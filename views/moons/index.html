Moons

What are moons?

Moons are natural satellites — objects that orbit planets or other celestial bodies. Most planets in our solar system have at least one moon, and some have dozens. Even dwarf planets like Pluto have moons!

How do moons form?

Moons form in a few different ways. Some moons are created at the same time as their planet, forming from the same cloud of gas and dust in space. Others are captured later, when a planet's gravity pulls in a passing asteroid or object. A third way happens when a large collision smashes into a planet, throwing debris into space that eventually clumps together to form a moon. Earth's Moon likely formed this way, after a massive impact with a Mars-sized object billions of years ago.

Moons in our solar system

There are hundreds of moons in our solar system, and they come in all shapes and sizes. Earth has just one large moon, but other planets have many. Jupiter has more than 90 moons, including Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system. Saturn also has dozens of moons, with Titan being one of the most interesting because it has thick clouds and lakes of liquid methane. Mars has two tiny, potato-shaped moons called Phobos and Deimos. Even dwarf planets like Pluto have moons — Pluto's largest moon, Charon, is so big that it makes Pluto wobble as they orbit each other. Some moons are rocky, some are icy, and a few may even have oceans hidden under their surfaces, making them exciting places to study in the search for life.
