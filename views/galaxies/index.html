Galaxies

What is a Galaxy?

A galaxy is a massive system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. Galaxies are the fundamental building blocks of the universe, and they come in a variety of shapes, sizes, and compositions.

There are three main types of galaxies based on their shape:

  • Spiral galaxies, like our own Milky Way, have a flat, rotating disk with spiral arms and a central bulge.
  • Elliptical galaxies range from nearly spherical to elongated ovals and generally contain older stars with less gas and dust.
  • Irregular galaxies lack a clear shape and are often chaotic in appearance, sometimes due to past collisions or interactions.

Galaxies can contain millions to trillions of stars, along with planetary systems, star clusters, nebulae, and supermassive black holes at their centers. The Milky Way, for example, contains over 100 billion stars and a black hole estimated to be about 4 million times the mass of our Sun.

Galaxies are not isolated—they exist in groups and clusters, and even larger structures called superclusters. They can collide and merge over billions of years, leading to dramatic changes in their structure and star formation.

As telescopes and observational technology improve, astronomers continue to discover more about the origins, evolution, and diversity of galaxies. These discoveries help us better understand the large-scale structure of the universe and our place within it.

Galaxies in the Universe

Galaxies are vast collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity. They are the grand cities of the cosmos, each containing millions to trillions of stars. There are estimated to be over two trillion galaxies in the observable universe, ranging from small dwarf galaxies to giant ellipticals that span hundreds of thousands of light-years.

Our own galaxy, the Milky Way, is a barred spiral galaxy about 100,000 light-years in diameter. It has several arms spiraling out from its center, where a supermassive black hole resides. The Milky Way contains over 100 billion stars, including our Sun, and is part of a small group of galaxies called the Local Group.

Galaxies are not static—they move, interact, and sometimes collide. In about 4 billion years, the Milky Way is expected to merge with its neighbor, the Andromeda Galaxy, creating a new, larger galaxy. These cosmic interactions shape the structure and evolution of galaxies over billions of years.

Galaxies are essential to understanding the universe’s history and future. By studying their formation, movement, and composition, astronomers gain insight into how the universe has evolved since the Big Bang.
